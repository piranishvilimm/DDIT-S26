# Christensen's Disruptive Innovation Framework

## Why this matters

The term "disruption" is abused constantly. Christensen's framework gives a stricter way to evaluate whether a challenger is truly disruptive or simply innovative, fast-growing, or technologically impressive.

## Three categories

### 1. Sustaining innovation
Incumbents improve products for existing customers. Performance rises along dimensions already valued by the mainstream market.

### 2. Low-end disruption
A challenger enters at the low end of an existing market by serving overserved customers with a simpler, cheaper, or more convenient offering.

### 3. New-market disruption
A challenger serves non-consumers or creates a new market where incumbents were not meaningfully competing.

## Core mechanism

Incumbents often focus on sustaining innovation because that is where their best customers and margins sit. This can leave openings for entrants to begin below the mainstream market or outside it.

## Questions to ask

- Who is the initial target customer?
- Is the entrant serving overserved customers?
- Is the entrant making the product accessible to non-consumers?
- Does the incumbent have an incentive to ignore the entrant?
- Is performance improving toward the mainstream market over time?

## Weekend 1 application

Use this framework to evaluate Uber, Airbnb, and Tesla. The value of this comparison lies partly in the fact that the classification is debatable. That is good. It forces precise reasoning.
