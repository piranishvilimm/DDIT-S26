# Rogers' Digital Transformation Model

## Core idea

David Rogers argues that digital transformation is not mainly about technology procurement. It is about strategic adaptation across five domains reshaped by digital technologies.

## The five domains

### 1. Customers
Customers are no longer passive endpoints. They are connected, informed, vocal, and often participants in brand, platform, and product ecosystems.

Questions:
- How are customer expectations changing?
- How is customer power shifting?
- What new channels or touchpoints matter?

### 2. Competition
Competition no longer stays inside neat industry borders. Platform firms, adjacent entrants, and digitally enabled challengers cross category lines.

Questions:
- Who now competes with incumbents?
- Are nontraditional entrants reshaping the market?
- Is the competitive set widening?

### 3. Data
Data is not exhaust. It is a strategic asset. Digital firms learn from user behavior, operations, and ecosystems continuously.

Questions:
- What data matters most?
- Who captures it?
- How does data improve decisions or lock in advantage?

### 4. Innovation
Innovation becomes iterative and continuous. Digital products evolve through testing, updates, and feedback loops rather than long fixed cycles.

Questions:
- How quickly can the firm test and adapt?
- Is innovation centralized or distributed?
- How does learning occur?

### 5. Value
Digital transformation often changes what customers pay for, how value is delivered, and where profits come from.

Questions:
- What is the core value proposition now?
- What part of the chain is being commoditized?
- What new revenue logic is emerging?

## How to use this framework in the course

Teams should apply the five domains to their client industry context. The aim is not to fill boxes mechanically. The aim is to surface where structural change is strongest and how those changes connect to strategy.
